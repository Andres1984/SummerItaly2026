# Summer School 2026  
## Causal Inference and Data Science for Smart Cities

This summer course introduces students to the use of **data science, causal reasoning, and urban analytics** to understand complex problems in smart cities. The course is designed as an applied learning experience where students move from exploratory data analysis to causal interpretation, using real and synthetic urban datasets related to mobility, road safety, infrastructure, environmental conditions, and decision-making in cities.

The main objective of the course is to show that data science in smart cities should not be limited to describing patterns or building predictive models. Instead, students will learn how to ask better questions about **why urban problems occur**, how different variables are connected, and how public interventions may generate different outcomes depending on the context.

Throughout the course, students will work with Python notebooks and practical case studies. They will explore urban datasets, construct visualizations, identify relevant variables, build decision trees, discuss causal diagrams, and compare naive analyses with more structured causal approaches. Special attention will be given to geospatial reasoning, allowing students to understand how urban problems are distributed across space and how location can influence the interpretation of data.

The course combines conceptual discussion with hands-on exercises. Students will learn how to distinguish between correlation and causation, identify confounders, mediators, and colliders, and use these ideas to improve the quality of data-driven decisions in smart cities. The course also emphasizes the role of data governance, contextual knowledge, and responsible interpretation when using artificial intelligence and machine learning for urban policy.

By the end of the course, students will be able to analyze smart city problems using a more rigorous and critical data science perspective. They will understand how causal thinking can support better decisions in areas such as traffic safety, urban mobility, infrastructure planning, environmental risk, and public service design.

## Course Topics

- Introduction to smart cities and urban data science  
- Exploratory data analysis for urban problems  
- Visualization of patterns in mobility, safety, and infrastructure  
- Geospatial analysis and spatial interpretation  
- Correlation versus causation in urban decision-making  
- Causal diagrams and causal reasoning  
- Confounders, mediators, and colliders  
- Decision trees as tools for intervention analysis  
- Comparison between naive and adjusted models  
- Responsible use of AI and data in smart cities  

## Learning Outcomes

At the end of the course, students will be able to:

- Understand the role of data science in smart city decision-making.  
- Explore and visualize urban datasets using Python.  
- Interpret geospatial patterns in city data.  
- Identify causal questions behind urban problems.  
- Build and interpret causal diagrams.  
- Recognize confounding, mediation, and collider structures.  
- Compare descriptive, predictive, and causal approaches.  
- Use decision trees to evaluate possible urban interventions.  
- Communicate data-driven findings for public and organizational decisions.  

## Methodology

The course follows a practical and progressive methodology. Each session combines short conceptual explanations with guided Python laboratories. Students will work step by step with notebooks, datasets, visualizations, and applied exercises. The emphasis is not only on running code, but also on interpreting results and connecting them with real urban decision-making problems.

The final goal is for students to develop a clear understanding of how data science and causal inference can be used together to support smarter, more transparent, and more responsible decisions in urban environments.
